\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate the two engines on the same fact streams, the same query set,
and the same verification harness. Every benchmark result reported below has
passed cross-engine semantic equivalence checking
(Section~\ref{sec:correctness}); rows for which the two engines produced
divergent canonical result sets would be flagged and excluded, and no such
divergence occurred in the runs reported here.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval:setup}

The measurements were taken on a single Windows~11 machine with an
\texttt{\showcpu}\ processor, \texttt{\showmemory}\ of memory, and Python
$3.14$. Both engines use in-memory SQLite storage; no on-disk paths are
exercised. Each latency figure is the arithmetic mean of ten warm-cache
runs following two warm-up iterations, all on the same fact stream and
query set. The dataset generator is seeded (default seed $42$) so that the
generated facts, entities, contexts, and query anchors are reproducible.
Configuration, git commit, and \texttt{uv.lock} hash are recorded in the
per-run manifest shipped with the artifact.

The dataset generator produces synthetic facts of arity in the range
$3$--$8$ with a controlled context depth and an entity distribution drawn
from a Zipf-like law that mirrors the skew typical of real knowledge graph
data. We report results at three scales: $100$, $1\,000$, and $10\,000$
facts. We do not report results at $10^5$ or $10^6$ facts, and the paper
does not extrapolate to those scales.

We classify queries into three families whose performance profiles are
fundamentally different:

\begin{description}
    \item[LOOKUP:] retrieve a fact or a small set of facts anchored at a
          specific entity or a specific context. These are the queries for
          which stalk indexing is designed.
    \item[GLOBAL:] full-scan queries with no context or entity anchor.
          These force the engine to touch every stored fact.
    \item[TEMPORAL:] range queries over the temporal envelope of a fact.
          Both engines rely on an auxiliary interval index for these.
\end{description}

The remainder of this section reports results by family. Numeric values are
means over ten runs; all confidence intervals were tight ($<10\%$ of the
mean) and are omitted from the main tables for readability.

\subsection{Insert Throughput}
\label{sec:eval:insert}

Insert throughput is nearly identical between the two engines. SFDB is
slightly faster because it stores the fact atomically as a single section,
while the KG engine must decompose the fact into $k + 4$ triples and update
three indexes per triple.

$1\,000$ & $59.0$  & $54.1$  & $0.92\times$ \\

\end{table}

Both engines scale linearly with dataset size, at approximately
$60\,\mu\text{s}$ per fact. The observed constant-factor advantage of SFDB
diminishes at scale as the shared serialisation and canonicalisation cost
dominates the per-fact overhead.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/latency.pdf}
\caption{Query latency across scales, log axis. SFDB LOOKUP is

\label{fig:latency}

Two things are visible in the table. First, LOOKUP performance diverges in
SFDB's favour by a factor that grows with scale: at $10\,000$ facts, SFDB is
$30\times$ faster because the stalk index returns a complete fact for the
anchor entity in a single hash lookup, while the KG engine must locate every
triple that mentions the entity through its POS index, gather them by event
identifier, and reassemble the fact through joins. Second, GLOBAL
performance diverges in the KG engine's favour by a factor that also grows
with scale, and much more sharply: at $10\,000$ facts, SFDB is $1\,405\times$
slower on an unrestricted scan. The reason is straightforward: the current
SFDB implementation answers a GLOBAL query by enumerating every open set in
the topology and merging the sections it finds, whereas the KG engine
simply scans its triple table. We return to the design consequences of
this in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Scalability}
\label{sec:eval:scalability}

Figure~\ref{fig:scalability} plots latency against dataset size on log axes.
Insert latency scales linearly for both engines. SFDB LOOKUP scales
sub-linearly because the stalk index is $O(1)$ in the number of facts and
the observed growth is dominated by fixed overhead (canonicalisation,
result serialisation). SFDB GLOBAL scales super-linearly in the number of
open sets, which is itself super-linear in the number of facts under our
synthetic generator, producing the sharp divergence visible in the plot.

\subsection{Cross-Engine Verification}
\label{sec:eval:verification}

Every benchmark row above was accompanied by a semantic equivalence check.
The check canonicalises both engines' result sets and asserts equality as
sets of canonical facts. Over the full evaluation ($10$ workloads $\times\ 3$
scales $\times\ 10$ runs $=\ 300$ measurements per engine) we observed no
divergence. This does not, on its own, prove Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence};
the theorem is proved constructively in Section~\ref{sec:correctness}. It
does, however, provide a comprehensive empirical corroboration on all the
queries and inputs we tested.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:eval:summary}

Three findings summarise the evaluation:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inserts are essentially equivalent}: SFDB is $0.88$--$0.96\times$
          KG latency, a marginal difference explained by the absence of
          reification overhead.

    \item \textbf{LOOKUP queries are $3$--$30\times$ faster on SFDB}: the
          stalk index resolves entity anchors in $O(1)$ hash lookups
          against the KG baseline's $O(k)$ triple reconstruction.

    \item \textbf{GLOBAL queries are up to $1\,405\times$ slower on
          SFDB}: the current implementation iterates open sets instead of
          maintaining a flat fact index. We treat this as a negative result
          and describe the remedy in Section~\ref{sec:future:global-index}.
\end{enumerate}

We do not claim SFDB is universally faster than the KG baseline; the
tradeoff is genuine, and the paper's contribution is to identify it, quantify
it, and describe the shape of the remedy.

\subsection{Limitations of the Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval:limitations}

We compare SFDB against a KG baseline that we implemented ourselves. That
baseline is not a production RDF store, and we make no claim about how SFDB
would fare against Apache Jena TDB2, Virtuoso, or GraphDB at production
scale. Integrating a standard RDF store adapter and reporting like-for-like
numbers on LUBM~\cite{guo2005lubm} or WatDiv is future work
(Section~\ref{sec:future}). Results at $10^4$ facts are indicative and
reproducible; results at $10^5$ or $10^6$ facts are not reported.
