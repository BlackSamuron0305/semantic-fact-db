\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Results}
\label{sec:discussion:interpretation}

The experimental results confirm our three hypotheses.
SheafDB outperforms the KG baseline on contextual queries because
context-indexed section storage avoids the join overhead inherent in
reified triple representations. For non-contextual queries involving
highly interconnected entities, the KG's triple indexes provide more
efficient access patterns.

\subsection{When SheafDB Excels}
\label{sec:discussion:sheaf-wins}

SheafDB is preferred when:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Queries are scoped to a specific context or context subtree.
    \item Facts have high arity ($k \geq 4$), where reification overhead
          becomes significant.
    \item Frequent global reconstruction from local sections is required.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{When KG Excels}
\label{sec:discussion:kg-wins}

The KG representation is preferred when:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Facts are predominantly binary relations.
    \item The graph is dense with many interconnections between entities.
    \item The dataset is flat (single context), providing no locality benefit.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:discussion:threats}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Implementation bias}: Both engines were implemented by the
          same authors. The KG baseline may not reflect the performance of
          mature systems like Jena or Virtuoso.
    \item \textbf{Dataset bias}: Synthetic datasets may not capture all
          characteristics of real-world knowledge graphs.
    \item \textbf{Query bias}: The benchmark query set may favor one
          representation over another.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Negative Results}
\label{sec:discussion:negative}

SheafDB's storage overhead grows with the number of cross-context references.
For datasets where most facts reference entities in multiple contexts, the
restriction map index approaches the KG's triple index in size, eliminating
the storage advantage.
