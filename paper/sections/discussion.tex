\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Results Do and Do Not Show}
\label{sec:discussion:interpretation}

The measurements in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} tell a two-sided story.
Where the query anchors an entity and the engine can answer it through
a single hash lookup into the stalk index, SFDB is between $3\times$ and
$30\times$ faster than a reified triple store on comparable hardware and at
comparable engineering effort. Where the query has no anchor and the engine
must produce every fact in the store, SFDB is up to three orders of magnitude
\emph{slower} than the KG baseline because it enumerates open sets rather
than a flat table. These two facts are not in tension: they are two sides of
the same decision. Once one commits to indexing by context, one must decide
what to do when a query does not use that index. The current implementation
does the naive thing; the remedy is straightforward and outlined below.

We do not claim, and the data do not support, that the sheaf-inspired
approach dominates a mature RDF store on every workload at every scale. Our
KG baseline is a research prototype implemented by the same team as the
sheaf engine and running against the same test harness, storage backend,
and canonicalisation pipeline. It is a controlled reference, not a
state-of-the-art. Any claim about how SFDB would fare against Jena TDB2 or
Virtuoso must wait for a comparable evaluation, which we identify as future
work.

\subsection{When the Design Helps}
\label{sec:discussion:sheaf-wins}

The context-indexed design helps when the workload has any of the
following properties. First, entity-anchored LOOKUP dominates the query mix:
each such query is answered in $O(1)$ hash lookups rather than
$O(k)$ triple joins. Second, facts have arity above two or three: reification
overhead grows linearly with arity in the KG baseline, while the SFDB engine
stores each fact atomically. Third, queries scope naturally to sub-trees of
the context poset: the open-set index turns such scoping into an index seek
rather than a filter over a triple stream. Fourth, the application requires
verifiable data integrity across representations: the canonical layer and
cross-engine verification harness make it cheap to run the same fact
stream through both engines and catch inconsistencies.

\subsection{When the KG Baseline Helps}
\label{sec:discussion:kg-wins}

The reified triple representation wins in the workloads that the SFDB
design was not built for. Unrestricted GLOBAL scans that must touch every
fact are one such case: a flat triple table is the natural structure for
that access pattern, and the current SFDB implementation pays a substantial
penalty. Workloads that are essentially binary---where facts genuinely are
subject-predicate-object triples with no context, no arity, and no
provenance---get no benefit from the sheaf structure and only pay the
overhead of maintaining the extra indexes. Finally, integration with the
extensive existing RDF and SPARQL tooling ecosystem is currently only
possible on the KG side.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:discussion:threats}

We identify four threats that a careful reader should weigh.

\begin{description}
    \item[Implementation bias.] Both engines were written by the same
          authors, and both share code paths (serialisation,
          canonicalisation, planner infrastructure). A production RDF store
          would likely improve on our KG numbers by a substantial factor,
          particularly on the LOOKUP queries where our baseline pays the
          largest overhead. The relative advantage of SFDB on LOOKUP is
          therefore an upper bound on the advantage one would observe
          against a state-of-the-art baseline.

    \item[Dataset bias.] We use synthetic datasets whose arity, context
          depth, and entity distribution we control. Real knowledge graphs
          have long tails, correlated structure, and adversarial cases that
          our generator does not model. Repeating the evaluation on
          LUBM~\cite{guo2005lubm} and DBpedia is the natural next step.

    \item[Query bias.] Our query set was designed with both engines in
          mind. It includes queries where each engine is expected to do
          well and where each is expected to struggle. A different query
          mix would shift the aggregate numbers, but the qualitative
          story---stalk indexing helps LOOKUP, open-set iteration hurts
          GLOBAL---would not change without a design change.

    \item[Scale.] All measurements are at $10^4$ facts or below. Standard
          RDF benchmarks start at $10^5$ triples. Our design choices are
          consistent with the complexity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:complexity},
          but empirically our claims are bounded at $10^4$.
\end{description}

\subsection{Negative Results}
\label{sec:discussion:negative}

Two negative results deserve to be stated plainly, because they discipline
the paper's overall claim.

The first is the GLOBAL scan penalty of up to $1\,405\times$ at $10\,000$
facts. It arises from an implementation choice (open-set enumeration
instead of a flat fact index) and is not intrinsic to the sheaf-inspired
design. Section~\ref{sec:future:global-index} sketches a fix. Until that fix
lands, SFDB is not an appropriate choice for workloads dominated by
unrestricted scans.

The second is the vacuousness of the sheaf condition in our setting. On a
finite poset with the Alexandrov topology, every presheaf is automatically a
sheaf; locality and gluing hold because the covers under consideration are
trivial. The system is, strictly, a presheaf database with a defensive
consistency check. This does not diminish the practical value of the design:
what makes the design work is the context poset, the stalk index, the
canonical intermediate representation, and the disciplined restriction
vocabulary that comes with them. It does mean that any framing of the paper
as a sheaf-theoretic breakthrough would be dishonest, and we do not adopt
that framing.
