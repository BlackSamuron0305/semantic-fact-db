We investigate whether sheaf theory provides a more efficient representation
for semantic facts than traditional knowledge graphs (KGs).
Standard KGs decompose $n$-ary semantic events into binary triples via
reification, increasing join complexity and storage overhead.
We propose a sheaf-based representation where semantic facts are sections
over a finite topological space, and queries are answered via restriction
maps rather than graph traversal.
We implement both a realistic KG baseline and a sheaf database (SheafDB),
prove their semantic equivalence via a canonical intermediate representation,
and benchmark them on identical workloads.
Our results show that SheafDB reduces join complexity for contextual queries
but incurs higher storage overhead for highly interconnected facts.
We provide theoretical guarantees for locality, gluing, and global section
reconstruction, and discuss the conditions under which each representation
is preferred.
