\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Scalability Limits}
\label{sec:limits:scalability}

The current implementation is single-node and in-memory.
Global section computation requires $O(|\mathcal{C}| \cdot N^2)$ time in
the worst case, which limits scalability to datasets where $|\mathcal{C}|$
and $N$ are moderate (on the order of $10^4$ facts).  All results in this paper were obtained at scales up to 10,000 facts.  Behaviour at larger scales is extrapolated from complexity analysis and is the subject of ongoing work.

\subsection{Mathematical Assumptions}
\label{sec:limits:math}

The sheaf model assumes that the context poset is finite and has the
Alexandrov topology. This is appropriate for our setting but may not
generalize to infinite or continuous context spaces. The gluing condition
assumes compatible sections can always be identified, which may fail in
the presence of contradictory information.

\subsection{Implementation Limitations}
\label{sec:limits:implementation}

The current prototype does not support:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Concurrent access or transactions.
    \item Persistent storage (in-memory only).
    \item Distributed execution.
    \item Streaming or incremental fact ingestion.
    \item Full SPARQL compliance (custom query language).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Benchmark Limitations}
\label{sec:limits:benchmark}

The benchmark suite covers 15 query types. While representative, this is
not exhaustive. Real-world workloads may exhibit different access patterns.
The synthetic datasets, while controlled, may not capture all nuances of
organic knowledge graph data.
